## Learning objectives

After this lesson, you should see the prior as a natural part of inference, know how to translate verbal knowledge into a simple prior, and understand when weak or strong priors make sense.

> **Mini example.** Before launching a new campaign, a marketing team believes the click-through rate is usually around $$2\%$$ to $$5\%$$ and rarely above $$10\%$$. That is exactly the kind of information a prior is meant to encode.
>
> **Quick check.** If you already know a parameter cannot be negative or cannot exceed 1, is that already enough information to shape a prior?

## Opening idea: we rarely start from zero

Suppose you are evaluating a new ad campaign. Before seeing this week's data, you already know that past campaigns usually had click rates around $$2\%$$ to $$5\%$$, rates above $$10\%$$ are rare, and rates extremely close to 0 are also unlikely for a well-targeted audience.

Bayesian inference says that ignoring all of that would be wasteful. The prior is the place where we encode that earlier knowledge clearly.

![What a prior means]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/what_is_prior.png)

## 1. What is a prior?

The prior $$P(\theta)$$ is the distribution describing what we believe about $$\theta$$ before seeing the current data.

In Bayes' theorem:

$$
P(\theta \mid D) \propto P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta),
$$

the prior is the starting point, the likelihood is the data's voice, and the posterior is the updated belief.

## 2. Where can priors come from?

### 2.1. Previous data

Priors can come from very ordinary sources such as pass rates from earlier semesters, defect rates from previous production batches, or conversion rates from earlier campaigns.

### 2.2. Domain knowledge

They can also come from domain knowledge, for example when a doctor knows what treatment success ranges are realistic, when an engineer knows that a sensor cannot drift too far, or when a teacher knows that a pass rate near $$99\%$$ is unrealistic for a very hard course.

### 2.3. Logical or physical constraints

Another source is logical or physical constraint, such as the fact that probabilities must lie in $$[0,1]$$, variances must be positive, and waiting times cannot be negative.

### 2.4. Cautious defaults when little is known

Even when we do not know very much, we usually still know something minimal. We may know, for instance, that a probability cannot be negative, that a defect rate is unlikely to be as high as $$95\%$$ if the product is still on the market, or that an extremely large regression coefficient would be implausible in the application domain. That is already information, and it is already enough to shape a prior.

![Why we need priors]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/why_need_prior.png)

## 3. Turning words into a prior

Suppose the marketing team says:

> “The conversion rate is probably around $$20\%$$, rarely below $$10\%$$, and unlikely to exceed $$35\%$$.”

Since the parameter is a probability, a Beta prior is natural. Beta$$(8,32)$$ has mean:

$$
\frac{8}{8+32} = 0.2.
$$

This prior says that the center is around $$0.2$$, that nearby values such as $$0.15$$ and $$0.25$$ remain plausible, and that very extreme values receive less support.

## 4. Same mean, different confidence

Put four priors side by side and the message becomes clearer: Beta$$(1,1)$$ stays almost flat, Beta$$(5,5)$$ leans gently toward $$0.5$$, Beta$$(20,80)$$ centers around $$0.2$$ with moderate confidence, and Beta$$(100,400)$$ stays around $$0.2$$ much more tightly.

![An almost flat prior on [0,1]]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_flat_uniform.png)

![A prior gently centered around 0.5]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_centered_half.png)

![A lower-centered prior with moderate concentration]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_centered_low_moderate.png)

![A lower-centered prior with strong concentration]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_centered_low_strong.png)

So a prior communicates two things at once: where the center is, and how strongly we believe in that center.

## 5. Common types of priors

![A spectrum from weak to strong priors]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/prior_types_spectrum.png)

### 5.1. Very weak or flat priors

Example: Beta$$(1,1)$$ for a probability.

This leaves a great deal of room for the data to dominate.

### 5.2. Weakly informative priors

Example: Beta$$(2,2)$$.

These rule out unreasonable extremes without forcing a strong opinion.

![Weakly informative prior example]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/weakly_informative_prior_example.png)

### 5.3. Strongly informative priors

Example: Beta$$(50,50)$$.

These carry substantial previous knowledge and can strongly influence the posterior when new data are scarce.

![Strong prior example]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/strongly_informative_prior_example.png)

## 6. Mixture priors: when one prior is not enough

So far, we have mostly acted as if prior knowledge could be summarized by a single family with a single center. In practice, however, prior knowledge can sometimes reflect genuine uncertainty between multiple plausible scenarios rather than uncertainty inside only one scenario.

Suppose a team is trying to infer a purchase rate $$\theta$$ for a new campaign. The operations team believes the campaign may fall into one of two regimes: either it will be fairly weak, with a rate around $$10\%$$, or it will be fairly strong, with a rate around $$30\%$$, and before seeing current data both possibilities remain genuinely plausible.

Instead of forcing that belief into one single Beta prior, we can use a mixture prior:

$$
p(\theta) = w_1 p_1(\theta) + w_2 p_2(\theta), \qquad w_1 + w_2 = 1.
$$

For example,

$$
p(\theta) = 0.6 \,\text{Beta}(2,18) + 0.4 \,\text{Beta}(6,14).
$$

Read in words, this means that before seeing the current data we place prior probability $$0.6$$ on a weaker scenario and prior probability $$0.4$$ on a stronger scenario, while still using a familiar Beta distribution inside each scenario to describe uncertainty about $$\theta$$.

Once data arrive, the posterior does two things at once: it updates each component prior in the usual way, and it also shifts the relative weights of the scenarios according to which one the data support more strongly. That is the key difference between a single prior and a mixture prior. With a single prior, the data update the parameter; with a mixture prior, the data update both the parameter and the relative credibility of competing prior stories about the world.

## 7. Why priors help in practice

### 7.1. They stabilize small datasets

If you flip a coin 3 times and get 3 heads, MLE gives $$\hat{\theta}=1$$. That is too extreme for so little data. A reasonable prior pulls the inference back toward a more cautious conclusion.

![Likelihood using only the observed 3 successes out of 3]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_regularization_likelihood.png)

![A Beta(2,2) prior pulling the inference away from an extreme conclusion]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_regularization_prior.png)

![The posterior after combining the weak dataset with the prior]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_prior_regularization_posterior.png)

### 7.2. They let knowledge accumulate

Yesterday's posterior can become today's prior. This matches how science and decision-making actually work.

### 7.3. They make assumptions visible

Instead of hiding assumptions, Bayesian analysis writes them down explicitly.

## 8. Prior predictive checks

A good prior should not only sound reasonable at the parameter level. It should also generate reasonable fake data.

The idea is to draw $$\theta$$ from the prior, simulate data from the model, and then check whether the resulting fake outcomes look plausible.

![Prior predictive check]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/prior_predictive_check.png)

If a prior for a class pass rate keeps generating absurd outcomes, then the prior needs revision.

## 9. Sensitivity analysis

A healthy Bayesian habit is to ask:

**If I change the prior a little, do my conclusions change a lot?**

![Prior sensitivity with weak data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/prior_sensitivity_weak_data.png)

With little data, different reasonable priors can matter more.

![Prior sensitivity with strong data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/prior_sensitivity_strong_data.png)

With strong data, reasonable priors often lead to similar posteriors.

## 10. Common objections

### 10.1. “Priors are too subjective”

All analyses contain assumptions. Priors simply make some of them visible and testable.

![On subjectivity and priors]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/addressing_subjectivity.png)

### 10.2. “Let the data speak for themselves”

Data never speak without a model. Choosing the model is already an assumption.

### 10.3. “Strong priors are unfair”

They are only problematic when poorly justified or poorly reported. If strong prior information is real, using it can be entirely appropriate.

## 11. Practical rules for choosing priors

A sensible workflow usually starts from the story of the problem rather than from a formula, then matches the distribution family to the parameter's domain, explains the prior in words before fixing its parameters, runs prior predictive checks, performs sensitivity analysis, and finally reports the prior choice clearly enough that someone else could question or revise it.

## Summary

**A prior is where Bayesian analysis stores background knowledge in a transparent way.** A good prior reflects what is known, respects the parameter's domain, generates plausible fake data, and survives reasonable sensitivity checks.

> **3 key takeaways.** A prior is not a random guess, but a way to encode background knowledge, constraints, or uncertainty before the current data; a good prior should be understandable in plain language rather than only in mathematical notation; and prior predictive checks together with sensitivity analysis are essential habits for evaluating priors.
