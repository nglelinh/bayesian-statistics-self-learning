## Learning objectives

After this lesson, you should see the posterior as the central object of Bayesian inference, know how it combines prior and likelihood, and know how to read it through intervals, probabilities, and predictions.

> **Mini example.** You start with a prior belief that a class pass rate is around $$60\%$$. The new class has 31 passes out of 40 students. The posterior is the updated belief after combining those two sources of information.
>
> **Quick check.** Why does the posterior usually not match the prior exactly, but also not necessarily match the raw observed proportion exactly?

## Opening idea: after seeing data, what should we believe?

Suppose you are tracking the pass rate of a new class. Before grading, you already have a prior based on previous semesters. Once the current data arrive, you should not ignore the old knowledge, but you should not ignore the new evidence either.

The posterior is the principled answer to that tension, because it is the place where prior knowledge and new evidence are forced to meet under a coherent probabilistic rule rather than through ad hoc judgment.

![Posterior as an updating machine]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/bayesian_update_machine_intro.png)

## 1. What is the posterior?

The posterior is the distribution of $$\theta$$ after observing data $$D$$:

$$
P(\theta \mid D).
$$

Bayes' theorem gives:

$$
P(\theta \mid D) = \frac{P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta)}{P(D)}.
$$

Or more compactly:

$$
\text{posterior} \propto \text{likelihood} \times \text{prior}.
$$

## 2. A step-by-step example

Suppose the prior is Beta$$(2,2)$$ and the data are 7 heads out of 10 tosses.

The Binomial likelihood contributes:

$$
P(D \mid \theta) \propto \theta^7(1-\theta)^3.
$$

The prior contributes:

$$
P(\theta) \propto \theta^{2-1}(1-\theta)^{2-1}.
$$

Multiplying gives:

$$
P(\theta \mid D) \propto \theta^8(1-\theta)^4,
$$

which is Beta$$(9,5)$$.

![From prior to posterior]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/prior_to_posterior.png)

The key interpretation is simple but important: the prior centered the inference around $$0.5$$, the data pushed it toward $$0.7$$, and the posterior ended up in between, around $$0.643$$, as a principled compromise rather than a casual average.

## 3. What is the posterior telling us?

When reading a posterior, it is useful to ask four connected questions: where its center lies, how wide it is, whether it is skewed, and what its tails imply about meaningful kinds of risk.

## 4. Why the posterior is richer than a point estimate

With the posterior, we can answer questions such as what the probability is that $$\theta > 0.5$$, what range contains most of the plausible values, and what kinds of outcomes might appear in future data.

![How prior and likelihood combine]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/combining_information.png)

## 5. Common summaries of the posterior

### 5.1. Posterior mean

$$
E[\theta \mid D].
$$

This is often the natural summary when we want one representative value and the decision problem treats squared error as especially costly.

### 5.2. Posterior median

The value that splits posterior mass into two equal halves.

It is often a more stable summary when the posterior is skewed and a central point is still needed.

### 5.3. Posterior mode or MAP

The value with highest posterior density.

It answers the question of which single value is most strongly supported at the peak of the posterior density.

![Posterior summary statistics]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/posterior_point_estimates.png)

These summaries can be close, but they need not be identical.

## 6. Credible intervals

A Bayesian $$95\%$$ credible interval $$[a,b]$$ satisfies:

$$
P(a \le \theta \le b \mid D) = 0.95.
$$

That means:

**Given the data and the prior, there is a $$95\%$$ probability that $$\theta$$ lies in that interval.**

![Credible intervals on the posterior]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/posterior_credible_intervals.png)

## 7. Credible interval versus confidence interval

### 7.1. Bayesian credible interval

Directly about the parameter:

$$
P(a \le \theta \le b \mid D) = 0.95.
$$

### 7.2. Frequentist confidence interval

About the long-run behavior of an interval-generating procedure, not the probability that the specific parameter lies in the specific interval.

![Credible intervals versus confidence intervals]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/credible_intervals_vs_confidence.png)

This is why credible intervals usually feel more natural to interpret.

## 8. How prior and data negotiate

An important intuition is that the posterior always reflects a negotiation between prior strength and data strength: a strong prior with little data tends to keep the posterior closer to the prior, a weak prior with abundant data lets the posterior move closer to the likelihood, and a strong prior confronted with strong data often produces a narrow posterior located in a compromise region.

## 9. Sequential updating

Bayesian updating is consistent over time. You can update once using all data at once, or update in stages by using today's posterior as tomorrow's prior, and the final result is the same so long as the model itself stays the same.

![Sequential Bayesian updating]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/sequential_updating.png)

## 10. Posterior predictive distributions

The posterior does not only tell us about the parameter. It also supports prediction for future data.

Examples include future sales, future pass counts, and future call volumes, which is why posterior predictive reasoning is often closer to the actual decisions people care about than the posterior of a parameter alone.

![Posterior predictive distribution]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/posterior_predictive.png)

This is where Bayesian inference becomes immediately useful for decision-making.

## 11. Common misunderstandings

### 11.1. “The posterior is just the normalized likelihood”

Not necessarily. The prior matters too, especially with limited data.

### 11.2. “The posterior mean is the one true answer”

Not always. Sometimes intervals, threshold probabilities, or predictive distributions matter more.

### 11.3. “Once the posterior looks nice, we are done”

No. A nice posterior cannot rescue a poor model. Model checking still matters.

## Summary

**The posterior is the updated belief after combining prior knowledge and data.** It is central because it lets us compute probabilities about parameters, build credible intervals, summarize uncertainty, and make predictions about future observations. The lasting lesson is that the posterior is not merely an intermediate formula, but the main object from which Bayesian reasoning becomes actionable: it combines old knowledge with new evidence, supports direct probability statements about parameters, and becomes most useful when it is used to answer practical questions about intervals, thresholds, and future outcomes.
