## Learning objectives

After this lesson, you should understand likelihood as a tool for **comparing parameter values using data**, avoid confusing it with the posterior, and know how to write a likelihood from a simple generative story.

> **Mini example.** You flip a coin 10 times and observe 7 heads. Does this dataset sound more reasonable under $$\theta=0.5$$ or under $$\theta=0.7$$?
>
> **Quick check.** Is likelihood about the probability of the data, or the probability of the parameter?

## Opening question: which hypothesis does the data support?

Suppose you flip a coin 10 times and observe 7 heads. The key questions are:

- If $$\theta = 0.5$$, how reasonable is this dataset?
- If $$\theta = 0.7$$, how reasonable is it?
- If $$\theta = 0.1$$, how reasonable is it?

Likelihood is exactly the tool that answers those questions.

![Likelihood as the data's story under each hypothesis]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_data_story.png)

## 1. What is likelihood?

With observed data $$D$$, the likelihood of $$\theta$$ is:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D) = P(D \mid \theta).
$$

The important change in viewpoint is this:

- the data are now fixed,
- the parameter varies,
- and we ask which values of the parameter make the observed data more plausible.

## 2. Coin example: 7 heads out of 10

If $$\theta$$ is the probability of heads, then:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D) = \binom{10}{7}\theta^7(1-\theta)^3.
$$

This tells us:

- values near $$0.7$$ fit the data well,
- values near $$0.5$$ fit the data moderately well,
- values near $$0.1$$ fit the data very poorly.

![Likelihood for 7 heads out of 10 tosses]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_function.png)

## 3. Likelihood is about relative support

The most useful way to read likelihood is comparatively.

For example:

$$
\frac{\mathcal{L}(0.7 \mid D)}{\mathcal{L}(0.5 \mid D)} \approx 2.28.
$$

So the observed data support $$\theta = 0.7$$ about 2.28 times more strongly than $$\theta = 0.5$$.

![Likelihood ratios compare hypotheses]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_ratio.png)

This is the key point:

- likelihood is not a probability distribution over the parameter,
- it is a scoring rule for how well parameter values explain the observed data.

## 4. Likelihood is not the probability of the parameter

This confusion is extremely common.

### 4.1. Probability view

If $$\theta$$ is fixed and the data vary, then $$P(D \mid \theta)$$ is a probability for data.

### 4.2. Likelihood view

If the data are fixed and $$\theta$$ varies, then the same expression is read as likelihood.

![Likelihood versus probability]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_vs_probability_concept.png)

Why is it not a probability distribution over $$\theta$$? Because its integral over $$\theta$$ usually does **not** equal 1.

That is fine. Likelihood was never meant to be a probability distribution over the parameter.

## 5. Real-world examples

### 5.1. Recovery after treatment

Suppose 12 out of 15 patients recover. If $$\theta$$ is the recovery probability:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D) = \binom{15}{12}\theta^{12}(1-\theta)^3.
$$

Values of $$\theta$$ near $$0.8$$ are strongly supported, while values near $$0.2$$ are not.

![Likelihood for recovery data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/binomial_likelihood_recovery.png)

### 5.2. Calls per hour

Suppose a call center receives 8 calls in one hour. If $$\lambda$$ is the mean number of calls per hour and the model is Poisson:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\lambda \mid D) = \frac{\lambda^8 e^{-\lambda}}{8!}.
$$

![Likelihood for Poisson count data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/poisson_likelihood_calls.png)

### 5.3. Student heights

Suppose you measure several student heights and want to infer the class mean $$\mu$$ under a Normal model. The likelihood tells you which mean values make the observed measurements more plausible.

![Likelihood for Normal measurement data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/normal_likelihood_heights.png)

## 6. How to build a likelihood

Use this three-step process:

1. Tell the data story.
2. Choose a generative model.
3. Write $$P(D \mid \theta)$$ and read it as a function of $$\theta$$.

Examples:

- successes out of $$n$$ trials  $$\rightarrow$$ Binomial,
- counts over time  $$\rightarrow$$ Poisson,
- continuous noisy measurements  $$\rightarrow$$ Normal.

## 7. Multiple observations

If observations are conditionally independent given the parameter:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid y_1,\dots,y_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(y_i \mid \theta).
$$

This means each new observation contributes more evidence.

## 8. Why do we use log-likelihood?

With many observations, likelihood becomes a product of many small numbers. So we often use:

$$
\ell(\theta \mid D) = \log \mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D)
= \sum_{i=1}^n \log P(y_i \mid \theta).
$$

This is easier to compute and optimize.

![Likelihood versus log-likelihood]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_log_likelihood_comparison.png)

## 9. Maximum likelihood estimation

The value that maximizes likelihood is the maximum likelihood estimate:

$$
\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \arg\max_{\theta}\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D).
$$

For Binomial data:

$$
\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \frac{k}{n}.
$$

MLE is useful, but it gives only a point estimate and does not include prior information.

## 10. Likelihood inside Bayes' theorem

In Bayesian inference:

$$
P(\theta \mid D) \propto P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta).
$$

Likelihood is the part that lets the data speak. Prior is the part that brings in background knowledge. Posterior is their combination.

## 11. Common misunderstandings

### 11.1. “High likelihood means high probability of the parameter”

Not yet. You need the posterior for a probability statement about the parameter.

### 11.2. “Likelihood is only a Bayesian idea”

No. Frequentist methods also rely heavily on likelihood, especially MLE.

### 11.3. “The highest likelihood value is the whole answer”

No. It is only a point summary. Bayesian inference keeps the full updated distribution.

## Summary

**Likelihood is how the data score competing parameter values.** It is not the posterior, and it is not a probability distribution over the parameter. It is the data's contribution to Bayesian updating.

> **3 key takeaways.**
> 1. Likelihood fixes the data and varies the parameter to see which values explain the observed data better.
> 2. The most useful reading of likelihood is relative comparison, not an absolute number.
> 3. Likelihood is a core ingredient of Bayes, but it is not yet the posterior.
