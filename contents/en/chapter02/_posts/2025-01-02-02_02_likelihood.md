## Learning objectives

After this lesson, you should understand likelihood as a tool for **comparing parameter values using data**, avoid confusing it with the posterior, and know how to write a likelihood from a simple generative story.

> **Mini example.** You flip a coin 10 times and observe 7 heads. Does this dataset sound more reasonable under $$\theta=0.5$$ or under $$\theta=0.7$$?
>
> **Quick check.** Is likelihood about the probability of the data, or the probability of the parameter?

## Opening question: which hypothesis does the data support?

Suppose you flip a coin 10 times and observe 7 heads. The key questions are not vague questions about the data in isolation, but comparative questions of the following form: if $$\theta = 0.5$$, how reasonable is this dataset; if $$\theta = 0.7$$, how reasonable is it; and if $$\theta = 0.1$$, does the same dataset now look highly implausible?

Likelihood is exactly the tool that answers those questions.

![Likelihood as the data's story under each hypothesis]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_data_story.png)

## 1. What is likelihood?

With observed data $$D$$, the likelihood of $$\theta$$ is:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D) = P(D \mid \theta).
$$

The important change in viewpoint is that the data are now treated as fixed, the parameter is allowed to vary, and we ask which values of the parameter make the observed data more plausible.

## 2. Coin example: 7 heads out of 10

If $$\theta$$ is the probability of heads, then:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D) = \binom{10}{7}\theta^7(1-\theta)^3.
$$

This tells us that values near $$0.7$$ fit the data well, values near $$0.5$$ fit the data moderately well, and values near $$0.1$$ fit the data very poorly.

![Likelihood for 7 heads out of 10 tosses]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_function.png)

### 2.1. Reading this example numerically

If we compute a few values directly, we get:

- $$\mathcal{L}(0.7 \mid D) \approx 0.267$$
- $$\mathcal{L}(0.5 \mid D) \approx 0.117$$
- $$\mathcal{L}(0.9 \mid D) \approx 0.057$$

This gives a very concrete interpretation. The data 7 heads out of 10 support $$\theta=0.7$$ about $$2.28$$ times more strongly than $$\theta=0.5$$, and about $$4.65$$ times more strongly than $$\theta=0.9$$. In other words, observing 7/10 does not mean the true parameter must be exactly $$0.7$$; it means values around that region explain the observed data much better than some other candidate values.

## 3. Likelihood is about relative support

The most useful way to read likelihood is comparatively.

For example:

$$
\frac{\mathcal{L}(0.7 \mid D)}{\mathcal{L}(0.5 \mid D)} \approx 2.28.
$$

So the observed data support $$\theta = 0.7$$ about 2.28 times more strongly than $$\theta = 0.5$$.

![Likelihood ratios compare hypotheses]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/likelihood_ratio.png)

This is the key point: likelihood is not a probability distribution over the parameter, but rather a scoring rule for how well different parameter values explain the observed data.

## 4. Likelihood is not the probability of the parameter

This confusion is extremely common.

### 4.1. Probability view

If $$\theta$$ is fixed and the data vary, then $$P(D \mid \theta)$$ is a probability for data.

### 4.2. Likelihood view

If the data are fixed and $$\theta$$ varies, then the same expression is read as likelihood.

![Likelihood as a function of the parameter with fixed data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_likelihood_concept.png)

![Posterior probability is what lets us talk directly about parameter uncertainty]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_posterior_probability_concept.png)

Why is it not a probability distribution over $$\theta$$? Because its integral over $$\theta$$ usually does **not** equal 1.

That is fine. Likelihood was never meant to be a probability distribution over the parameter.

## 5. Real-world examples

### 5.1. Recovery after treatment

Suppose 12 out of 15 patients recover. If $$\theta$$ is the recovery probability:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D) = \binom{15}{12}\theta^{12}(1-\theta)^3.
$$

Values of $$\theta$$ near $$0.8$$ are strongly supported, while values near $$0.2$$ are not.

![Likelihood for recovery data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/binomial_likelihood_recovery.png)

### 5.2. Calls per hour

Suppose a call center receives 8 calls in one hour. If $$\lambda$$ is the mean number of calls per hour and the model is Poisson:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\lambda \mid D) = \frac{\lambda^8 e^{-\lambda}}{8!}.
$$

![Likelihood for Poisson count data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/poisson_likelihood_calls.png)

### 5.3. Student heights

Suppose you measure several student heights and want to infer the class mean $$\mu$$ under a Normal model. The likelihood tells you which mean values make the observed measurements more plausible.

![Likelihood for Normal measurement data]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/normal_likelihood_heights.png)

## 6. How to build a likelihood

A reliable way to build a likelihood is to move in three steps. First, tell the data story as clearly as possible: are you counting successes and failures, counting events over time, or measuring a continuous quantity with noise? Second, choose a generative model that actually matches that story, such as Binomial for successes out of $$n$$ trials, Poisson for counts over time, or Normal for continuous noisy measurements. Third, write $$P(D \mid \theta)$$ and then read it as a function of $$\theta$$ rather than as a probability distribution over the parameter.

## 7. When the data-generating mechanism matters

In many problems, writing the correct likelihood is not only about choosing a familiar distribution family. It also depends on how the information was generated or revealed.

### 7.1. Success on the third attempt

Suppose a student keeps taking an exam until passing, and the observed data are “the student passed on the third attempt.” If $$\theta$$ is the pass probability on each attempt, the actual data story is that the first two attempts were failures and the third attempt was the first success. That means the likelihood is

$$
P(Y=3 \mid \theta) = \theta(1-\theta)^2.
$$

If we incorrectly read this as a Binomial problem of “1 success in 3 trials,” we have already written the wrong likelihood. What matters is the temporal structure of the observation.

### 7.2. Sampling without replacement

Suppose balls are drawn from a box without replacement. Then the likelihood for the next draw depends not only on the hypothesis about the box, but also on the history of previous draws. The observations are no longer conditionally independent in the same way they would be under a Binomial model, because the probabilities change after each draw.

### 7.3. Information revealed strategically

In problems such as Monty Hall, the data are not merely “a door was opened.” What matters is that the host knows where the prize is and deliberately opens a door hiding a goat. If we ignore that mechanism, we write the wrong model. The general lesson is that likelihood depends not only on the final observed outcome, but also on the rule that generated or revealed that outcome.

## 8. Multiple observations

If observations are conditionally independent given the parameter, then the total likelihood is the product of the contribution from each observation:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid y_1,\dots,y_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(y_i \mid \theta).
$$

This means that each new observation contributes additional evidence, so as more data accumulate the likelihood often becomes sharper and more informative.

## 9. Why do we use log-likelihood?

With many observations, likelihood becomes a product of many small numbers. So we often use

$$
\ell(\theta \mid D) = \log \mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D)
= \sum_{i=1}^n \log P(y_i \mid \theta).
$$

This is easier to compute, easier to optimize, and numerically more stable.

![Likelihood values can become numerically tiny with larger datasets]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_likelihood_curve_large_n.png)

![Log-likelihood keeps the same comparison structure while staying easier to compute with]({{ site.baseurl }}/img/chapter_img/chapter02/chapter02_log_likelihood_curve_large_n.png)

## 10. Maximum likelihood estimation

The value that maximizes likelihood is the maximum likelihood estimate:

$$
\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \arg\max_{\theta}\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid D).
$$

For Binomial data:

$$
\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \frac{k}{n}.
$$

MLE is useful, but it gives only a point estimate and does not include prior information, which is exactly why Bayesian analysis refuses to stop there.

## 11. Likelihood inside Bayes' theorem

In Bayesian inference,

$$
P(\theta \mid D) \propto P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta).
$$

Likelihood is the part that lets the data speak, prior is the part that brings in background knowledge, and posterior is the result of combining the two. If likelihood remains conceptually unclear, posterior will almost always remain unclear as well.

## 12. Common misunderstandings

### 12.1. “High likelihood means high probability of the parameter”

Not yet. You need the posterior for a probability statement about the parameter.

### 12.2. “Likelihood is only a Bayesian idea”

No. Frequentist methods also rely heavily on likelihood, especially MLE.

### 12.3. “The highest likelihood value is the whole answer”

No. It is only a point summary. Bayesian inference keeps the full updated distribution.

## Summary

**Likelihood is how the data score competing parameter values.** It is not the posterior, and it is not a probability distribution over the parameter. It is the data's contribution to Bayesian updating.

> **3 key takeaways.** Likelihood fixes the data and varies the parameter to see which values explain the observed data better; the most useful reading of likelihood is relative comparison rather than an absolute number; and likelihood is a core ingredient of Bayes, but it is not yet the posterior.
