Chapter 2 develops the technical core of Bayesian inference. If Chapter 1 motivates why Bayesian reasoning is a sensible way to think about statistics, Chapter 2 explains how that updating process is written mathematically through probability distributions, likelihood, prior, and posterior.

In this chapter, you will learn the distinct role of each part of a Bayesian model. Probability distributions describe uncertainty in the quantities we care about; the likelihood tells us which parameter values are better supported by the observed data; the prior encodes background knowledge or initial constraints; and the posterior combines these two sources of information into an updated belief. The lessons on conjugate priors and grid approximation make this updating process especially concrete before you move on to more complex models later in the course.

The goal of the chapter is not only to help you memorize Bayes' rule, but to understand what each term means in modeling practice. Once you are comfortable with this chapter, you will have the foundation needed to read, build, and evaluate Bayesian models in a systematic way.
